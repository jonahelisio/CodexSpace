Regions

The three primary outputs

Negative region

Samples for which f(x, y, z) < -epsilon. This is the negative side of the sampled field and one of the primary sign-defined volumes in the decomposition.

Surface band

Samples for which |f(x, y, z)| <= epsilon. This is the near-zero tolerance band surrounding the implicit zero-set and acts as the field-native surface layer.

Positive region

Samples for which f(x, y, z) > +epsilon. This is the positive side of the sampled field and complements the negative region across the surface band.

Method

Why this is modeling

Field Engine begins with scalar fields rather than explicit polygon meshes. A bounded domain is sampled, each point is assigned a field value, and the resulting lattice is classified by sign and near-zero proximity. Geometry emerges from the structure of the field itself: sign separation, zero-crossing behavior, and downstream reconstruction from classified samples.

In this phase of the project, the three outputs are not decorative variants. They are distinct regions of a single field and can be studied independently, exported independently, or recombined as a technical and visual model of one offspring expression.

Collaboration

Alternate cover introductions

This project is not being presented as solitary authorship. Its current workflow depends on sustained collaboration with AI models: iteration, correction, smoke tests, reframing, disagreement, recovery, and the productive tension between intention and interpretation. The three versions below treat that collaboration as part of the method, not an invisible convenience.

Version 1

Technical / formal

Field Engine was developed as a collaborative system for exploring 3D form through scalar fields, sign structure, implicit surfaces, and reconstruction workflows. I built the engine with jonahelisio so he could work beyond conventional mesh-first modeling and investigate how spatial form can emerge from sampled mathematical fields.

Jonahelisio came to this work through foundry layout, bench practice, CNC programming, and industrial modeling tools such as Mastercam, AutoCAD, Rhino, and Inventor. That background established a strong sensitivity to interfaces, cavities, mounting surfaces, registration, and part relationships. Field Engine extends those instincts into a field-native modeling process where positive region, negative region, and surface band are discovered classifications of space rather than pre-drafted components.

This site documents that transition and the collaborative method behind it. The work advances through repeated iteration with AI models: proposing structures, stress-testing outputs, refining language, running smoke tests, recovering from malformed offspring, and pushing the field system into new territory.

Version 2

Research / poetic hybrid

Field Engine began as a way to compute form instead of drawing it directly. I built the system for jonahelisio so he could move through 3D space by means of fields, thresholds, offspring formulas, and reconstruction. What emerged was not only a tool for modeling, but a method for watching structure appear, split, collapse, recover, and take on meaning.

Jonahelisio’s eye was shaped in manufacturing: foundry layout, CNC programming, and years inside Mastercam, AutoCAD, Rhino, and Inventor. That discipline remains visible here. Even when the work becomes abstract, it still searches for body, housing, interface, seam, cavity, support, and relation. The field is not treated as noise. It is treated as material.

The process is collaborative in the fullest sense. AI models are not hidden assistants behind the page. They are part of the working pressure of the project: drafting, misreading, correcting, provoking, clarifying, and helping pull the river of the work into new channels. This site is a record of those interactions as much as it is a record of the images.

Version 3

Personal / narrative

I built Field Engine with jonahelisio so he could explore a much wider creative expanse of 3D space than conventional modeling tools usually allow. The goal was not simply to make shapes. It was to create a system where formulas, fields, sign changes, surface bands, and reconstruction could all become part of one evolving modeling language.

Jonahelisio started from bench layout in the foundry and moved through CNC operation and programming into digital modeling with Mastercam, AutoCAD, Rhino, and Inventor. That history matters. It is why the outputs of this system are often read not just as abstractions, but as bodies, housings, mounts, interfaces, and assemblies. The manufacturing eye remains present even when the geometry comes from cross-bred fields.

The work shown here was developed through extensive collaboration with AI models. That collaboration is not clean or one-directional. It includes iteration, smoke tests, misunderstandings, reformulations, and the back-and-forth tension that produces stronger results than either side would likely reach alone. This page is one marker in that shared process.
